			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Pen</h1>

<p>A <b>pen</b> (<a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>: <i>penna</i>, feather) is a <a href="/wiki/Writing_implement" title="Writing implement">writing implement</a> used to apply <a href="/wiki/Ink" title="Ink">ink</a> to a surface, such as <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a>, for <a href="/wiki/Writing" title="Writing">writing</a> or <a href="/wiki/Drawing" title="Drawing">drawing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Historically, <a href="/wiki/Reed_pen" title="Reed pen">reed pens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quill" title="Quill">quill</a> pens, and <a href="/wiki/Dip_pen" title="Dip pen">dip pens</a> were used, with a <a href="/wiki/Nib_(pen)" title="Nib (pen)">nib</a> dipped in ink. <a href="/wiki/Ruling_pen" title="Ruling pen">Ruling pens</a> allow precise adjustment of line width, and still find a few specialized uses, but <a href="/wiki/Technical_pen" title="Technical pen">technical pens</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Rapidograph" class="mw-redirect" title="Rapidograph">Rapidograph</a> are more commonly used. Modern types also include <a href="/wiki/Ballpoint_pen" title="Ballpoint pen">ballpoint</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rollerball_pen" title="Rollerball pen">rollerball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fountain_pen" title="Fountain pen">fountain</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Marker_pen" title="Marker pen">felt</a> or <a href="/wiki/Porous_point_pen" class="mw-redirect" title="Porous point pen">ceramic tip</a> pens.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_pens">Types of pens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of pens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern">Modern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Modern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The main modern types of pens can be categorized by the kind of writing tip or point on the pen:</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:03-BICcristal2008-03-26.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/03-BICcristal2008-03-26.jpg/220px-03-BICcristal2008-03-26.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/03-BICcristal2008-03-26.jpg/330px-03-BICcristal2008-03-26.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/03-BICcristal2008-03-26.jpg/440px-03-BICcristal2008-03-26.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2512" data-file-height="1682" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:03-BICcristal2008-03-26.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An inexpensive ballpoint pen</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li>A <b><a href="/wiki/Ballpoint_pen" title="Ballpoint pen">ballpoint pen</a></b> dispenses ink by rolling a <a href="/wiki/Ball_bearing" title="Ball bearing">small hard sphere</a>, usually 0.5–1.2&#160;mm and made of <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">brass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tungsten_carbide" title="Tungsten carbide">tungsten carbide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The ink dries almost immediately on contact with paper. The ballpoint pen is usually reliable and comes in both inexpensive and expensive types. It has replaced the fountain pen as the most common <a href="/wiki/Tool" title="Tool">tool</a> for everyday writing.</li>
<li>A <b><a href="/wiki/Rollerball_pen" title="Rollerball pen">rollerball pen</a></b> dispenses a water-based liquid or <a href="/wiki/Gel_pen" title="Gel pen">gel</a> ink through a ball tip similar to that of a ballpoint pen. The less-viscous ink is more easily absorbed by paper than oil-based ink, and the pen moves more easily across a writing surface. The rollerball pen was initially designed to combine the convenience of a ballpoint pen with the smooth "wet ink" effect of a fountain pen. Gel inks are available in a range of colors, including <a href="/wiki/Metallic_paint" title="Metallic paint">metallic paint</a> colors, <a href="/wiki/Glitter" title="Glitter">glitter</a> effects, <a href="/wiki/Neon" title="Neon">neon</a>, blurred effects, <a href="/wiki/Colorfulness" title="Colorfulness">saturated</a> colors, <a href="/wiki/Pastel" title="Pastel">pastel</a> tones, <a href="/wiki/Vibrant" class="mw-redirect" title="Vibrant">vibrant</a> shades, <a href="/wiki/Shade_(color)" class="mw-redirect" title="Shade (color)">shady</a> colors, <a href="/wiki/Invisible_ink" title="Invisible ink">invisible ink</a>, <a href="/wiki/See-through" class="mw-redirect" title="See-through">see-through</a> effect, shiny colors, and glow-in-the-dark effects.</li>
<li>A <b><a href="/wiki/Fountain_pen" title="Fountain pen">fountain pen</a></b> uses water-based liquid <a href="/wiki/Ink" title="Ink">ink</a> delivered through a <i><a href="/wiki/Nib_(pen)" title="Nib (pen)">nib</a></i>. The ink flows from a reservoir through a "feed" to the nib, then through the nib, due to <a href="/wiki/Capillary_action" title="Capillary action">capillary action</a> and gravity. The nib has no moving parts and delivers ink through a thin slit to the writing surface. A fountain pen reservoir can be refillable or disposable, this disposable type being an <i>ink cartridge</i>. A pen with a refillable reservoir may have a mechanism, such as a piston, to draw ink from a bottle through the nib, or it may require refilling with an <a href="/wiki/Pipette" title="Pipette">eyedropper</a>. Refill reservoirs, also known as <i>cartridge converters</i>, are available for some pens which use disposable cartridges.</li>
<li>A <b><a href="/wiki/Marker_pen" title="Marker pen">marker</a></b>, or <b>felt-tip pen</b>, has a porous tip of fibrous material. The smallest, finest-tipped markers are used for writing on <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a>. Medium-tip markers are often used by children for coloring and drawing. Larger markers are used for writing on other surfaces such as corrugated boxes, <a href="/wiki/Whiteboard" title="Whiteboard">whiteboards</a> and for <a href="/wiki/Chalkboard" class="mw-redirect" title="Chalkboard">chalkboards</a>, often called "liquid chalk" or "chalkboard markers." Markers with wide tips and bright but <a href="/wiki/Transparency_and_translucency" title="Transparency and translucency">transparent</a> ink, called <i><a href="/wiki/Highlighter" title="Highlighter">highlighters</a></i>, are used to mark existing text. Markers designed for children or for temporary writing (as with a whiteboard or overhead projector) typically use <a href="/wiki/Non-permanent_marker" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-permanent marker">non-permanent inks</a>. Large markers used to label shipping cases or other packages are usually <a href="/wiki/Permanent_marker" title="Permanent marker">permanent markers</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historic">Historic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Historic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These historic types of pens are no longer in common use as writing instruments, but may be used by calligraphers and other artists:</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dip_Pen.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Dip_Pen.jpg/220px-Dip_Pen.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Dip_Pen.jpg/330px-Dip_Pen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Dip_Pen.jpg/440px-Dip_Pen.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dip_Pen.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A dip pen</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li>A <b><a href="/wiki/Dip_pen" title="Dip pen">dip pen</a></b> (or <i>nib pen</i>) consists of a metal <a href="/wiki/Nib_(pen)" title="Nib (pen)">nib</a> with <a href="/wiki/Capillary_action" title="Capillary action">capillary channels</a>, like that of a fountain pen, mounted on a handle or holder, often made of wood. A dip pen usually has no ink reservoir and must be repeatedly recharged with ink while <a href="/wiki/Drawing" title="Drawing">drawing</a> or <a href="/wiki/Writing" title="Writing">writing</a>. The dip pen has certain advantages over a fountain pen. It can use waterproof pigmented (particle-and-binder-based) inks, such as so-called <i><a href="/wiki/India_ink" title="India ink">India ink</a></i>, drawing ink, or <a href="/w/index.php?title=Acrylic_inks&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Acrylic inks (page does not exist)">acrylic inks</a>, which would destroy a fountain pen by clogging, as well as the traditional <a href="/wiki/Iron_gall_ink" title="Iron gall ink">iron gall ink</a>, which can cause <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> in a fountain pen. Dip pens are now mainly used in <a href="/wiki/Illustration" title="Illustration">illustration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calligraphy" title="Calligraphy">calligraphy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Comics" title="Comics">comics</a>. A particularly fine-pointed type of dip pen known as a <i>crowquill</i> is a favorite instrument of artists, such as <a href="/wiki/David_Stone_Martin" title="David Stone Martin">David Stone Martin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jay_Lynch" title="Jay Lynch">Jay Lynch</a>, because its flexible metal point can create a variety of delicate lines, textures and tones with slight pressures while drawing.</li>
<li>The <b><a href="/wiki/Ink_brush" title="Ink brush">ink brush</a></b> is the traditional writing implement in East Asian calligraphy. The body of the brush can be made from either bamboo, or rarer materials such as red sandalwood, glass, ivory, silver, and gold. The head of the brush can be made from the hair (or feathers) of a wide variety of animals, including the weasel, rabbit, deer, chicken, duck, goat, pig, tiger, etc. There is also a tradition in both China and Japan of making a brush using the hair of a newborn, as a once-in-a-lifetime souvenir for the child. This practice is associated with the legend of an ancient Chinese scholar who scored first in the Imperial examinations by using such a personalized brush. Calligraphy brushes are widely considered an extension of the calligrapher's arm. Today, calligraphy may also be done using a pen, but pen calligraphy does not enjoy the same prestige as traditional brush calligraphy.</li>
<li>A <b><a href="/wiki/Quill" title="Quill">quill</a></b> is a pen made from a <a href="/wiki/Flight_feather" title="Flight feather">flight feather</a> of a large bird, most often a <a href="/wiki/Goose" title="Goose">goose</a>. Quills were used as instruments for writing with ink before the metal dip pen, the fountain pen, and eventually the ballpoint pen came into use. Quill pens were used in <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a> times to write on <a href="/wiki/Parchment" title="Parchment">parchment</a> or paper. The quill eventually replaced the reed pen.</li>
<li>A <b><a href="/wiki/Reed_pen" title="Reed pen">reed pen</a></b> is cut from a reed or bamboo, with a slit in a narrow tip. Its mechanism is essentially similar to that of a quill. The reed pen has almost disappeared but it is still used by young school students in some parts of India and Pakistan, who learn to write with them on small timber boards known as "Takhti".</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:US68445.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/US68445.png/220px-US68445.png" width="220" height="254" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/US68445.png/330px-US68445.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f0/US68445.png 2x" data-file-width="333" data-file-height="385" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:US68445.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
M. Klein and Henry W. Wynne received US patent#68445 in 1867 for an ink chamber and delivery system in the handle of the fountain pen.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egyptians</a> had developed writing on <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">papyrus</a> scrolls when scribes used thin reed brushes or <a href="/wiki/Reed_pen" title="Reed pen">reed pens</a> from the <i><a href="/wiki/Juncus_maritimus" title="Juncus maritimus">Juncus maritimus</a></i> or sea rush.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In his book <i>A History of Writing</i>, Steven Roger Fischer suggests that on the basis of finds at <a href="/wiki/Saqqara" title="Saqqara">Saqqara</a>, the reed pen might well have been used for writing on parchment as long ago as the First Dynasty or about 3000 BC. Reed pens continued to be used until the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> although they were slowly replaced by quills from about the 7th century. The reed pen, generally made from bamboo, is still used in some parts of Pakistan by young students and is used to write on small boards made of timber.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The reed pen survived until papyrus was replaced by animal skins, vellum and parchment, as a writing surface. The smoother surface of skin allowed finer, smaller writing by the quill pen, derived from the flight feather.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Quill" title="Quill">quill pen</a> was used in Qumran, Judea to write some of the <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea_Scrolls" title="Dead Sea Scrolls">Dead Sea Scrolls</a>, which date back to around 100 BC. The scrolls were written in <a href="/wiki/Hebrew" class="mw-redirect" title="Hebrew">Hebrew</a> dialects with bird feathers or quills. After the fall of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, Europeans had difficulty in obtaining reeds<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and began to use quills. There is a specific reference to quills in the writings of <a href="/wiki/St._Isidore_of_Seville" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Isidore of Seville">St. Isidore of Seville</a> in the 7th century.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Quill pens were still widely used in the 18th century, and were used to write and sign the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Constitution of the United States">Constitution of the United States</a> in 1787.</p>
<p>A copper <a href="/wiki/Nib_(pen)" title="Nib (pen)">nib</a> was found in the ruins of <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a> showing that metal nibs were used in the year 79.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> There is also a reference to 'a silver pen to carry ink in', in <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Pepys" title="Samuel Pepys">Samuel Pepys</a>' diary for August 1663.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> 'New invented' metal pens are advertised in <i>The Times</i> in 1792.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> A metal pen point was patented in 1803 but the patent was not commercially exploited. A patent for the manufacture of metal pens was advertised for sale by <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Donkin" title="Bryan Donkin">Bryan Donkin</a> in 1811.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> John Mitchell of <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> started to mass-produce pens with metal nibs in 1822, and thereafter the quality of steel nibs had improved enough that dip pens with metal nibs came into generalized use.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>The earliest historical record of a pen employing a reservoir dates back to the 10th century. In 953, <a href="/wiki/Al_Muizz" class="mw-redirect" title="Al Muizz">Ma'ād al-Mu'izz</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Fatimid_Caliphate" title="Fatimid Caliphate">Fatimid Caliph</a> of <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, demanded a pen which would not stain his hands or clothes, and was provided with a pen which held ink in a reservoir and delivered it to the nib.<sup id="cite_ref-bosworth1981_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bosworth1981-12">[12]</a></sup> This pen may have been a fountain pen, but its mechanism remains unknown, and only one record mentioning it has been found. A later reservoir pen was developed in 1636. In his <i>Deliciae Physico-Mathematicae</i> (1636), <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">German</a> inventor <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Schwenter" title="Daniel Schwenter">Daniel Schwenter</a> described a pen made from two quills. One quill served as a reservoir for ink inside the other quill. The ink was sealed inside the quill with <a href="/wiki/Cork_(material)" title="Cork (material)">cork</a>. Ink was squeezed through a small hole to the writing point. In 1809, Bartholomew Folsch received a patent in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> for a pen with an ink reservoir.<sup id="cite_ref-bosworth1981_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bosworth1981-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>While a student in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romanian</a> <a href="/wiki/Petrache_Poenaru" title="Petrache Poenaru">Petrache Poenaru</a> invented the <a href="/wiki/Fountain_pen" title="Fountain pen">fountain pen</a>, which the <a href="/wiki/French_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="French Government">French Government</a> patented in May 1827. Fountain pen patents and production then increased in the 1850s.</p>
<p>The first patent on a ballpoint pen was issued on October 30, 1888, to <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_J_Loud&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="John J Loud (page does not exist)">John J Loud</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1938, <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A1szl%C3%B3_B%C3%ADr%C3%B3" title="László Bíró">László Bíró</a>, a Hungarian newspaper editor, with the help of his brother George, a chemist, began to work on designing new types of pens including one with a tiny <a href="/wiki/Ball" title="Ball">ball</a> in its tip that was free to turn in a socket. As the pen moved along the paper, the ball rotated, picking up ink from the ink cartridge and leaving it on the paper. Bíró filed a British patent on June 15, 1938. In 1940 the Bíró brothers and a friend, Juan Jorge Meyne, moved to <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> fleeing Nazi Germany and on June 10, filed another patent, and formed <i>Bíró Pens of <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a></i>. By the summer of 1943 the first commercial models were available.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Erasable ballpoint pens were introduced by <a href="/wiki/Papermate" class="mw-redirect" title="Papermate">Papermate</a> in 1979 when the <a href="/wiki/Erasermate" title="Erasermate">Erasermate</a> was put on the market.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Slavoljub_Eduard_Penkala" title="Slavoljub Eduard Penkala">Slavoljub Eduard Penkala</a>, a naturalized <a href="/wiki/Croats" title="Croats">Croatian</a> engineer and <a href="/wiki/Inventor" class="mw-redirect" title="Inventor">inventor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Poles" title="Poles">Polish</a>-<a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">Dutch</a> origin from the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Croatia-Slavonia" title="Kingdom of Croatia-Slavonia">Kingdom of Croatia-Slavonia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a>, became renowned for further development of the mechanical pencil (1906) – then called an "automatic pencil" – and the first solid-ink fountain pen (1907). Collaborating with an entrepreneur by the name of <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Moster" title="Edmund Moster">Edmund Moster</a>, he started the Penkala-Moster Company and built a pen-and-pencil factory that was one of the biggest in the world at the time. This company, now called <i>TOZ-Penkala</i>, still exists today. "TOZ" stands for "<i>Tvornica olovaka Zagreb</i>", meaning "<a href="/wiki/Zagreb" title="Zagreb">Zagreb</a> Pencil Factory".</p>

<p>In the 1960s, the fiber or <a href="/wiki/Felt-tipped_pen" class="mw-redirect" title="Felt-tipped pen">felt-tipped pen</a> was invented by Yukio Horie of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tokyo_Stationery_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tokyo Stationery Company (page does not exist)">Tokyo Stationery Company</a>, Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Papermate's Flair was among the first felt-tip pens to hit the U.S. market in the 1960s, and it has been the leader ever since. <a href="/wiki/Marker_pen" title="Marker pen">Marker pens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Highlighter" title="Highlighter">highlighters</a>, both similar to felt pens have become popular in recent times.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rollerball_pen" title="Rollerball pen">Rollerball pens</a> were introduced in the early 1970s. They make use of a mobile ball and liquid ink to produce a smoother line. Technological advances achieved during the late 1980s and early 1990s have improved the roller ball's overall performance. A <a href="/wiki/Porous_point_pen" class="mw-redirect" title="Porous point pen">porous point pen</a> contains a point that is made of some porous material such as felt or ceramic. A high quality drafting pen will usually have a ceramic tip, since this wears well and does not broaden when pressure is applied while writing.</p>
<p>Although the invention of the typewriter and <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">personal computer</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Keyboard_(computing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Keyboard (computing)">keyboard</a> input method have changed how users write, the pen has not been entirely replaced.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Higher end pens including fountain pens are still a <a href="/wiki/Status_symbol" title="Status symbol">status symbol</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
